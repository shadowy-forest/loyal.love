category theory is the study of structures and the relationships between them



like a model for models, it can provide a way to abstract away from the specifics of each structure and instead focus on the patterns and connections that link them.

basically, you look at 1ï¸âƒ£ a collection of objects (e.g., sets, groups, rings, fields, apples) and 2ï¸âƒ£ the morphisms between them (i.e., the arrows, each with a source and a target).

if you remember my previous post about group theory for astro patterns, you know that groups must satisfy four properties: associativity, identity, inverse, and closure.

in category theory, there are only two(-ish) main properties: composition (i.e., morphisms are associative) and identity (each object has a special morphism that acts neutrally under composition).

categories are super cool for science!
for example, in a very very very high level, functional programming (e.g., in languages like haskell) can be seen as a category where objects are types and morphisms are functions between them (i.e., the identity function and function composition satisfy beautifully the requirements of category axioms).

or, in quantum mechanics, categories can be used to describe systems and processes â€” here, objects might represent quantum systems, and morphisms are quantum operations or evolutions.

on machine learning frameworks
